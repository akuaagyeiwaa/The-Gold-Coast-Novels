Joseph Ephraim Casely-Hayford

Joseph Ephraim Casely Hayford, also known by his native name Ekra Agyiman, was born on September 28, 1866, and died in 1930. His father, Reverend Joseph de Graft Hayford‚Äîoriginally named Kwamina Affua‚Äîhad his name changed by Christian missionaries, reflecting the colonial imposition on indigenous identities (Ofosu-Appiah x). Casely Hayford began his formal education at Wesleyan High School in Cape Coast and later proceeded to Fourah Bay College in Freetown, Sierra Leone. He returned to teach at his alma mater and was subsequently appointed principal of the Wesleyan High School in Accra.

Casely Hayford‚Äôs intellectual pursuits extended beyond the classroom into journalism and the legal profession. He collaborated with Prince Brew of Dunkwa at the Western Echo press and, in 1893, joined the Gold Coast Chronicle as a sub-editor. He later served as the editor of the Gold Coast Echo. During this period, he also worked as a law clerk for Mr. Eiolart, a European lawyer based in Cape Coast.

In 1893, Casely Hayford traveled to England to study law at the Inner Temple. While in England, he also matriculated as a non-collegiate student at the University of Cambridge for a year. He was called to the bar at Inner Temple in 1896 and subsequently returned to the Gold Coast, where he practiced law in Cape Coast, Axim, Sekondi, and Accra.

His personal life was marked by both tragedy and resilience. His first marriage to Beatrice Madeline Pinnock produced one child, but she died in childbirth during the delivery of their second. He later married Gladys Smith of Freetown. Two of his children‚ÄîArchie Casely Hayford and Adelaide Casely-Hayford‚Äîsurvived him.
